What this page is, where every number comes from, and which bits happen on their own. Read the first section and you will understand the page. Keep reading if you want to know exactly how a figure was worked out.

1. The big picture

Money comes in from your properties and your work. Some goes out on living costs. What is left over is split into pots for the future.

2. The one rule that explains everything

Every personal spending category is EITHER a budget OR a pot. Never both. A budget is money that leaves as you spend it, so it reduces what's left over. A pot is money you set aside first and spend later, so spending it empties the pot but does not reduce what's left over.

Why that second half matters

Imagine you save £1,000 a month for tax. After five months the pot holds £5,000, the tax bill arrives, and you pay it.

If the tax bill counted as spendingWhat actually happens now
That month shows a £5,000 lossThe tax pot goes from £5,000 to £0
What's left over goes negativeWhat's left over is unaffected
Every other pot gets nothing that monthEvery other pot fills as normal

The pot existed to absorb that bill. Counting it twice would punish you for using it properly. So bucket spending is shown on the page, but never deducted.

The five pots

Each month every pot receives its percentage of what's left over. If nothing is left over, nothing goes in — pots never go backwards.

A pot empties when you spend on its category:

If a pot shows £0 with lots of spending against it, that is not a fault. It means you have spent more through that pot than what's left over has put in. Overspend carries forward, so the pot stays at £0 until later months make it up.

Credit card payments — the tricky one

Paying a card creates two records: money out of your current account, and money into the card. Counting both would double it. Counting the wrong one can miss it entirely.

1
Money arriving into a credit card is a payment. No labelling needed — this is read automatically. AUTOMATIC
2
For a closed card with no feed (Barclaycard, NatWest) there is no card record to read, so the current-account side is used instead. You label those Personal Credit Card Transfer when you reconcile. YOU
3
If both exist, the duplicate is removed. Each card record cancels at most one current-account record, so two same-value payments in one week are both still counted. AUTOMATIC
4
A bounced direct debit that comes back cancels itself out. AUTOMATIC

The same figure is used by the cash flow row and the Debt pot, from one shared calculation, so the two can never disagree.

Debts you cannot see

A closed card still owes money, and with no feed there is nothing to read. Rather than a number that goes stale, these are worked out: a known starting balance and date, its interest rate, and every payment found in your bank feed since.

DebtStarting pointHow payments are found
BarclaycardLast synced balance, Aug 2025Any transaction naming Barclaycard or the card number
NatWest cardStatement, June 2026Any transaction naming NatWest
Charging orderThe order itself, May 2026No payments — it only accrues

Each one moves on its own every month. Nothing to type in. AUTOMATIC

The charging order's interest rate is an assumption, not something taken from the paperwork. It is flagged in the record for confirmation. Treat that balance as an estimate.

If you remember one thing Budgets are money leaving now. Pots are money set aside for later. Keeping them apart is what makes every other number on this page true.
